Figure 1. Project Plan

OMV Internalisation


This is one of the most important areas of the project we hoped to model. The output from this step gives us clues if our experiments are theoretically sound. OMV internalisation is the process where the OMV goes into the cell and releases its contents. This is the most important step of the whole process as this determines the endocytosis will be successful or not. An OMV that binds to the receptor is internalised by allowing it to form a pouch in the cell membrane and the OMV enters the cell through this pouch and releases the prodrug inside the cell. Acro pinocytosis, clathrin mediated endocytosis, caveolin mediated endocytosis, or non‐caveolin, non clathrin mediated endocytosis. These pathways have all been implicated in mediating OMV entry into host cells. Clathrin coated pits are formed allowing OMVs to utilise toxin receptor mechanism to internalise the cargo. Different levels of purity in preparation of OMVs affects the efficiency of drug delivery. OMV internalisation was also mathematically modelled using one dimensional differential equation allowing us to attain greater accuracy in overall understanding of the mechanism. Initial vescicular association with the membrane can be descibed as given below \[C+C_{bs} \underset{k_{d}}{\stackrel{k_{a}}{\rightleftharpoons}} C_{b}\] Where \(C_{0}\) stands for the free vescicles, \(R_{s}\) for the number of free cell-surface receptors and \(C_{}\) for the number of vesicles with single ligand bound to surface receptor. \(k_{a1}\) and \(k_{d1}\) represent the association and dissociation rate constants \[C_{b} \overset{k_{int}}\to C_{i}\] \[\frac{dC_{b}}{dt} = k_{a}C_{bs}C - k_{d}C_{b} - k_{int}C_{b}\] \[\frac{C_{bs}}{dt} = -k_{a}C_{bs} + k_{d}C_{b} + R_{b}\] \[\frac{dC_{i}}{dt} = k_{int}C_{b}\]

Transcription and Translation


Transcription and Translation are the processes by which genetic information or instruction is passed on by cells during cell division. Transcription is the process in which a DNA strand is copied onto an RNA strand to encode proteins. The RNA polymerase binds to the promoter DNA and transcribes the messenger RNA which serves as the template strand for protein synthesis. The ribosome binds to this template strand during translation and codes for the respective protein. The quantification of transcription is important as it controls which proteins are to be expressed and at what rate. A proper model of Transcription and translation must take into account the various leakage factors and the initiation and termination rates during the various steps of the process. A transcription circuit coupled with translation circuit translated into a set of differential equations is what gives us the rate and relation between various elements factored in our model. This allows us to achieve a decently accurate picture of the genetic activity inside the cell. A main question we wanted to answer was about the rate of production of proteins inside the cell. By knowing our inputs precisely, how could we predict the rate of production of proteins inside the cell? To answer this question, we made a flow diagram of the genetic activity of our interest inside the cell. The diagram is as given below.

    4. Electroporation


    Electroporation is the method of using high voltage electric pulses to increase cell permeability facilitating delivery of drug and genetic material into the cell. In our project this technique has played a crucial role in loading the drug into the OMV’s which acts as the vehicle for drug delivery in this targeted model of delivery. The challenge was to optimize the size of the pore so as to enhance the rate of drug carried in each OMV without compromising the drug loading process. Hence a mathematical model was created to calculate the optimum pore size to be created and the most effective carrying capacity of each OMV. This was done with help of one-dimensional differential equations that took the pore size and carrying capacity as the variables. Protocols to be followed are to be carefully studied as serious damage to OMVs jeopardise the final aim of the drug delivery mechanism.


    Pore Density


    We assume the OMV's to be spherical in nature occupying a radius R. The electric field applied induces pores on the surface. Their exact size and radius distribution depends on the electric field being applied. One must be careful with this process, because any slight mistake could lead to irreversible electroporation and disruption of the membrane integrity. The pore density N is governed by the well known Krassova and Filev equation, given below: \[\frac{d N}{d t}=\alpha\exp\left(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{e p}}\right)^{2}\left[1-\frac{N}{N_{0}\exp\left(q\left(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{e p}}\right)^{2}\right)}\right]\]
    Where {V_{m}} is the transmembrane potential of the spherical cell in a uniform electric field defined as: \[{V_{m}} = 1.5E \times r_{c}cos\psi\]
    The equation then becomes: \[\frac{dN_{ep}}{dt} = K(1 - N_{ep}C)\] Solving the above ordinary differential equation yeilds us: \[-{\frac{ln(1 - N_{ep}C)}{C}}{\rvert}_{N_{epo}}^{{N_{ep}}^{t}}\] \[-KCt = ln{(\frac{1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}{1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C})}\] \[e^{-kct} = \frac{1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}{1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C}\] \[e^{-kct}{(1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C)} = {1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}\] The constants for our case are given below: \[\alpha = 1\times10^{9}\] \[V_{m} = 0.08\] \[V_{ep} = 0.258\] \[{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})} = 0.3100775194\] \[{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2} = 0.096148\] \[e^{{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2}} = 1.10092\] \[e^{-{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2}} = 1.10092\] \[r_{m} = 0.8\times{10}^{-9}m\] \[r_{a} = 0.51\times{10}^{-9}m\] \[\frac{r_{m}}{r_{\alpha}} = 1.5686\] \[q = 2.46059\] \[K = \alpha{e}^{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}} = 1.10092\] \[C = \frac{{e}^{-q{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}}}}{N_{o}}= {(\frac{0.789322}{N_{o}})}\] We plug in these values for our original equation.     Ligand Receptor Interaction


    Ligand receptor interactions are a major class of protein-protein interactions that regulate lifeprocesses and helps maintain specificity of various proteins and their functions. The conformationalchange that the ligand undergoes effectively acts as a lock and key mechanism which discriminatesbetween proteins and makes sure that only the specific ligand binds to the receptor site.Understanding ligand receptor interactions and quantifying their rates are crucial for designing the prodrug delivery mechanism and ensuring the selective targeting of cancerous cells with minimumcollateral damage to healthy cells. The rate at which ligand binds at the receptor mechanism and theprobability of a ligand failing to do so give us important information in deciding the optimum dosage ofthe pro drug to be loaded. It also helps in finding the rate of reaction and the time taken for thesuccessful completion of the binding. We proceed with finding the binding energies using Autodock Vina,a popular molecular docking software.


    Prodrug Enzyme Interaction


    Prodrug is the precursor drug that is inactive independently but activates itself as it combines with the promoiety. The damage to healthy cells is prevented by this inactive nature of prodrug, as the specific combination of proteins found on the cancerous cells will make sure that only pro drug and pro moiety enters the cell only in them and even if accidentally it enters in other cells only one of them enters and being inactive will cause no damage to the cell. The prodrug enzyme kinetics are quite important in understanding amount of prodrug required to destroy each cell and load it respectively. Chemical instability, poor water solubility, rapid pre systemic metabolism, inadequate absorption, and toxicity and many more hurdles in usual drug delivery mechanisms are overcome by the prodrug method. This method also has less toxicity and is efficient compared to other methods making it a suitable candidate for such processes.
