Mathematical modeling

Mathematical modeling plays a crucial role in understanding the project keeping precision l'esprit. For DUONCO, we segregated the entire project into its constituent steps to look at the avenues we had in hand.







We identified 7 areas where we could work on. Upon further counselling from experts, we realistically narrowed down our search to four. These avenues have been described below. We have also provided with a picture of our understanding as well as all the equations used and have cited the works we have used for achieving our goals

PROTEIN PRODUCTION

A main question we wanted to answer was about the rate of production of proteins inside the bacteria. By knowing our inputs precisely, how could we predict the rate of production of proteins inside the bacteria? This is important for us because the same quantity of protein will be expressed on the surface of the OMV, and we wanted to control this density. To answer this question, we initially made a flow diagram of the genetic activity of our interest inside the cell. The diagram is as given below.

Here,to get the Protein output vs IPTG concentration on the extracellular space, we try to take into account each and every metabolic process that could be realistically modeled within the time frame available to us. Our gene of interest is inserted into E.coli SKBR3 using the plasmid pGEX-4T-1. The plasmid we used in our case had the tac promoter in place for the lac operator that a normal lac operon model has. Though we included every factor we could think of in the model, we have done so based on the following assumptions

Assumptions

  • IPTG is transported by both LacY and by free diffusion

  • No catabolite repression as to LBbroth has no glucose

  • pTac drives transcription at a rate \(11 \times\)of LacUV5

  • LacI dimerizes and the dimer alone binds to the operator site LacO. We neglect tetramerization of LacI

  • LacI dimer binds with 1:1 stochiometry to LacO site

  • IPTG is not metabolized by LacZ and quation for LazZ are neglected

  • 2 IPTG molecules bind to a single dimer

  • Neglecting cell growth and cell division, DNA content stays the same for throughout

  • Copy number of pGEX4T1 with PBR322 origin is 20

  • Lac operon has other leakage factors that don't contribute significantly to the output

OMV INTERNALISATION

One of the avenues of modeling we were hoping to achieve was OMV internalization.OMV internalisation is the process where the OMV goes into the cell and releases its contents. This is one of the most important areas of the project we hoped to model. The output from this step gives us clues if our experiments are theoretically sound. OMV internalization is important for our project because it allows for the targeted delivery of the prodrug and enzyme to cancer cells that overexpress both HER2 and CX3CR1.

This system is designed to function like an AND gate, where the drug is only active in cells that overexpress both markers. Additionally, modeling OMV internalization can help to optimize the system for maximum efficacy and minimize side effects.. OMV internalisation was also mathematically modelled using one dimensional differential equation allowing us to attain greater accuracy in overall understanding of the mechanism. Initial vescicular association with the membrane can be descibed as given below \[C+C_{bs} \underset{k_{d}}{\stackrel{k_{a}}{\rightleftharpoons}} C_{b}\] Where \(C\) stands for the free vescicles, \(C_{bs}\) for the number of free cell-surface receptors and \(C_{b}\) for the number of vesicles with single ligand bound to surface receptor. \(k_{a}\) and \(k_{d}\) represent the association and dissociation rate constants Let the equation describing vesicular internalization given by the below equation \[C_{b} \overset{k_{int}}\to C_{i}\] The factors affecting the equilibrium reaction can be denoted as below \[\frac{dC_{b}}{dt} = k_{a}C_{bs}C - k_{d}C_{b} - k_{int}C_{b}\] Similarly, the free receptor change could be taken into account as given below \[\frac{C_{bs}}{dt} = -k_{a}C_{bs} + k_{d}C_{b}\] Internalization can be expressed by the equation below \[\frac{dC_{i}}{dt} = k_{int}C_{b}\]

ELECTROPORATION

Electroporation is the method of using high voltage electric pulses to increase cell permeability facilitating delivery of drug and genetic material into the cell. In our project this technique has played a crucial role in loading the drug into the OMV’s which acts as the vehicle for drug delivery in this targeted model of delivery. The challenge was to optimize the size of the pore so as to enhance the rate of drug carried in each OMV without compromising the drug loading process. Hence a mathematical model was created to calculate the optimum pore size to be created and the most effective carrying capacity of each OMV. This was done with help of one-dimensional differential equations that took the pore size and carrying capacity as the variables. Protocols to be followed are to be carefully studied as serious damage to OMVs jeopardise the final aim of the drug delivery mechanism.

Pore Density

We assume the OMV's to be spherical in nature occupying a radius R. The electric field applied induces pores on the surface. Their exact size and radius distribution depends on the electric field being applied. One must be careful with this process, because any slight mistake could lead to irreversible electroporation and disruption of the membrane integrity. The pore density N is governed by the well known Krassova and Filev equation, given below \[\frac{d N}{d t}=\alpha\exp\left(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{e p}}\right)^{2}\left[1-\frac{N}{N_{0}\exp\left(q\left(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{e p}}\right)^{2}\right)}\right]\]

Where {V_{m}} is the transmembrane potential of the spherical cell in a uniform electric field defined as: \[{V_{m}} = 1.5E \times r_{c}cos\psi\]

We isolate particulars of these equation using the below constants \[K = \alpha{e}^{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}}\] \[C = \frac{ {e}^{-q{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}}}}{N_{o}}\]

The equation then becomes: \[\frac{dN_{ep}}{dt} = K(1 - N_{ep}C)\] Solving the above ordinary differential equation yeilds us: \[-{\frac{ln(1 - N_{ep}C)}{C}}{\rvert}_{N_{epo}}^{{N_{ep}}^{t}}\] \[-KCt = ln{(\frac{1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}{1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C})}\] \[e^{-kct} = \frac{1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}{1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C}\] \[e^{-kct}{(1 - {N_{ep}}^{o}C)} = {1 - {N_{ep}}^{t}C}\] The constants for our case are given below: \[\alpha = 1\times10^{9}\] \[V_{m} = 0.08\] \[V_{ep} = 0.258\] \[{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})} = 0.3100775194\] \[{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2} = 0.096148\] \[e^{{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2}} = 1.10092\] \[e^{-{(\frac{V_{m}}{V_{p}})}^{2}} = 1.10092\] \[r_{m} = 0.8\times{10}^{-9}m\] \[r_{a} = 0.51\times{10}^{-9}m\] \[\frac{r_{m}}{r_{\alpha}} = 1.5686\] \[q = 2.46059\] \[K = \alpha{e}^{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}} = 1.10092\] \[C = \frac{ {e}^{-q{\frac{V_{m}^{2}}{V_{ep}^{2}}}}}{N_{o}}= {(\frac{0.789322}{N_{o}})}\] We plug in these values for our original equation. PRODRUG ENZYME INTERACTION

Prodrug is the precursor drug that is inactive independently but activates itself as it combines with the promoiety. The damage to healthy cells is prevented by this inactive nature of prodrug, as the specific combination of proteins found on the cancerous cells will make sure that only pro drug and pro moiety enters the cell only in them and even if accidentally it enters in other cells only one of them enters and being inactive will cause no damage to the cell. The prodrug enzyme kinetics are quite important in understanding amount of prodrug required to destroy each cell and load it respectively. Chemical instability, poor water solubility, rapid pre systemic metabolism, inadequate absorption, and toxicity and many more hurdles in usual drug delivery mechanisms are overcome by the prodrug method. This method also has less toxicity and is efficient compared to other methods making it a suitable candidate for such processes.

LIGAND RECEPTOR INTERACTION

Ligand receptor interactions are a major class of protein-protein interactions that regulate life processes and helps maintain specificity of various proteins and their functions. The conformational change that the ligand undergoes effectively acts as a lock and key mechanism which discriminates between proteins and makes sure that only the specific ligand binds to the receptor site. Understanding ligand receptor interactions and quantifying their rates are crucial for designing the pro drug delivery mechanism and ensuring the selective targeting of cancerous cells with minimum collateral damage to healthy cells. The rate at which ligand binds at the receptor mechanism and the probability of a ligand failing to do so give us important information in deciding the optimum dosage of the pro drug to be loaded. It also helps in finding the rate of reaction and the time taken for the successful completion of the binding. We proceed with finding the binding energies using Autodock Vina, a popular molecular docking software.
